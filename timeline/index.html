Timeline

By the year.

An annual retrospective. What I shipped, who I worked with, what changed.

2026

The throughline of 2026 is depth: rather than starting new things, Abhijit went further into the work he began in late 2025 — contributing to llama.cpp's WebGPU backend as a research assistant under Professor Tyler Sorensen and PhD student Reese Levine at UCSC.

The year opened with a focused push on the WebGPU backend's shader compilation architecture. Three pull requests landed in quick succession to ggml-org/llama.cpp: first, binary operators were migrated from ahead-of-time template expansion to just-in-time pre-WGSL compilation, with careful handling of buffer binding overlaps. Then a production bug surfaced — WebGPU's 65,535 workgroup-per-dimension limit was silently failing large matrix multiplication batches — fixed by introducing a 2D workgroup dispatch helper and bounds checking across all mul_mat kernels. By March, the AOT-to-JIT migration was complete, with the copy, GLU, RoPE, and soft_max operators all ported over.

In April, the work expanded to infrastructure: Abhijit built webgpu-bench, an automated end-to-end benchmarking framework that compiles llama.cpp to WebAssembly and drives real browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Safari) via Playwright to measure inference performance and numerical correctness across 10 models and 230 quantization variants. A static dashboard at webgpu-bench.abhijitramesh.me makes the results visible to the broader contributor community.

Alongside this, a subtle Metal compiler bug — discovered in the Q4_K mul_mat kernel on Chrome/Apple Silicon — was isolated into a minimal public repro and filed upstream to the Dawn WebGPU project, with both browser and native C++ test runners to narrow the root cause.

2026 so far is a portrait of systems-level open-source work: upstream contributions, tooling for the community, and rigorous debugging at the boundary of compilers and GPU hardware.

2025

2025 was a year of closing chapters and opening new ones. The first half was a wind-down and handoff: Abhijit wrapped up his work at a digital-health firm and a healthcare-AI startup, shipping a serverless multi-modal ensemble inference framework for the cardiac diagnostic device cardiac diagnostic device — combining signal-only and signal+spectrogram models across folds — and handing it off to the a medical-device manufacturer team for production. Simultaneously, the VR heart segmentation model he had built for pediatric congenital heart disease surgery planning went fully live, and he delivered a standalone inference service so clinicians at the collaborating hospital could run segmentations directly without touching the ML stack.

The second half marked a clean break: Abhijit moved to Santa Cruz, California, to begin his MS in Computer Science at UC Santa Cruz. By the end of fall quarter — his first — he had joined Professor Tyler Sorensen's research group, where he works alongside PhD student Reese Levine on the WebGPU backend for llama.cpp. The goal is to bring GPU-accelerated LLM inference to the browser and cross-platform environments. His early contributions include a merged fix for xielu parameter passing and an open PR adding WebGPU support for a set of unary operators (FLOOR, CEIL, ROUND, TRUNC, EXPM1, SOFTPLUS).

In short: 2025 was the year Abhijit shipped the last of his healthcare AI production work in India, relocated, and pivoted into systems-level ML research at the compiler/runtime layer.

2024

2024 was a year of pushing existing bets to production. Two long-running medical AI tracks — the the cardiac diagnostic device cardiac signal work at a digital-health firm and the an academic medical-AI lab / a healthcare-AI startup EUS platform — both hit meaningful milestones.

On the the cardiac diagnostic device side, Abhijit ground-up rewrote the ML training system into a proper production platform (PyTorch Lightning, Hydra, Optuna, W&B), expanded the model architecture space to include ResNet, Transformers, and tsai time-series models, and built a YOLO-based Valsalva maneuver detector as a preprocessing step for the classifier. The motivation was clear: an FDA submission was in flight, and the experiments needed to be reproducible, scalable, and traceable. Across 176 commits over nine months, Abhijit was the primary contributor through a rotating team. Most of the work landed.

At a healthcare-AI startup — funded by the a research institute's second consecutive annual grant ($180K in 2024) — the scope broadened significantly. The EUS endoscopy platform added double classification and expanded its station vocabulary. Both the eusml-labeller and liver-labeller received Tumor Timer and patient data encryption upgrades. A brand-new project appeared: automatic heart segmentation for congenital heart disease, where pediatric CT scans are segmented and loaded into a VR environment for surgical planning with a clinical collaborator — a system now in clinical use as of 2025. And a fresh data pipeline began feeding labeled EUS frames into CVAT for a pancreas segmentation model.

Two parallel tracks, both with real clinical stakes, both shipped.

2023

2023 was a year of operationalization and tooling expansion across two parallel tracks.

At a digital-health firm, the the cardiac diagnostic device PAD pipeline — built from the ground up in 2022 — shifted from research to production engineering. The year's 171 commits focused on automating the inference path: Lambda-triggered Step Functions state machines, SageMaker docker automation, SNS-based event routing, and shared cross-account S3/IAM infrastructure. The system grew from a research pipeline into a team-operable, event-driven MLOps platform before the engagement closed in November.

At a healthcare-AI startup, the EUS_ML model pipeline matured with frequency-domain preprocessing (Fourier transforms), a patient-level train/test/val split mandated by the clinical team (a clinical collaborator), and a PyTorch Lightning 2.0 upgrade. Alongside the model work, two new labeling web apps shipped: eusml-labeller for real-time annotation during pancreas endoscopy procedures, and liver-labeller for liver ultrasound elastography — both built in Remix and deployed by September. These replaced retrospective video review, putting labeling directly in the hands of clinicians at the point of care.

The year also marked an institutional milestone: a healthcare-AI startup received its first a research institute grant — $210,000 — recognizing the AI/ML research program that Abhijit had been building since 2021. It would renew in 2024 and 2025.

2022

2022 was the year Abhijit went deep on applied ML for healthcare. Working as an ML Engineer at a digital-health firm — subcontracted by a medical-device manufacturer — he spent the full year building the data quality and training pipeline for the cardiac diagnostic device, a medical device that detects Peripheral Arterial Disease from infrared blood volume waveforms. The work was genuinely full-stack: from signal preprocessing and self-supervised model research (trying TSBERT and time-series approaches before settling on the right modality), through Optuna hyperparameter search and Neptune experiment tracking, to a Dash labeling webapp backed by DynamoDB and a Lambda→S3→EC2 data pipeline on AWS. The project shipped. Alongside that, he made minor open-source contributions to PatchSorter, a computational pathology tool, as part of the same engagement — a small sign of the breadth a digital-health firm required of its engineers.

2021

2021 was a year of applied ML — moving from Android and open-source into machine learning research, with two substantively different projects bookending the year.

The first half was dominated by building the data foundation for a long-running medical AI project at the partner university. Under the banner of a healthcare-AI startup, the company registered to formalize the work, Abhijit engineered the full EUS video-to-dataset pipeline from scratch: extracting frames, filtering artifacts specific to endoscopic ultrasound (doppler overlays, GUI elements, colored pointer markings), generating station-level labels from spreadsheet metadata, and standing up an inference engine capable of running on both GPU and CPU. The goal was automatic pancreatic cancer detection — a high-stakes problem that would carry forward into subsequent years.

The second half brought a Zoho internship under Jayaraj Poroor, a principal engineer, working on a research problem at the intersection of symbolic reasoning and deep learning: using domain constraints to generate synthetic training data and encode domain knowledge directly into neural networks. The application domains were financial modeling and Zoho Sheets. The work was prototype-track and its downstream fate was unknown, but it introduced Abhijit to hybrid symbolic-statistical learning — a thread that sits at the frontier of ML research.

Together, 2021 marks the year the focus shifted decisively toward ML — one project in medical imaging, one in constrained learning — setting up a multi-year arc in healthcare AI.

2020

2020 was a year of consolidation and role reversal. The headline was flipping from GSoC contributor to GSoC mentor — returning to The Mifos Initiative, but this time guiding Garvit Agarwal through integrating Mifos Mobile CN with the Open Banking API layer via WSO2 API Gateway. Having built the same codebase from scratch the year before, Abhijit could mentor from genuine first-hand experience: he knew where the sharp edges were, what the architecture demanded, and what "done" actually looked like. The project passed, the PR landed, and the work earned him an invitation to the GSoC Mentor Summit 2020 — a selective recognition given to a small fraction of that year's mentors.

In parallel, the Bertelsmann Technology Scholarship from late 2019 converted into a full Udacity Machine Learning Engineer Nanodegree — months of working through SageMaker, XGBoost, deployment pipelines, and hyperparameter tuning. It was deliberate, independent upskilling, laying groundwork for what would come next.

2019

2019 was the year Abhijit went from open source contributor to recognized one. The year was anchored by Google Summer of Code with The Mifos Initiative — three months building the first functional version of Mifos Mobile CN, an Android banking app for unbanked populations in remote areas. The work was technically demanding in ways that weren't in the proposal: the codebase was mid-migration from Java to Kotlin, and getting both to coexist required going deep into interoperability. The offline-first constraint added another layer — the app needed to work in villages with no reliable internet, which shaped every architectural decision around data caching and state management. All 12 proposed features shipped. All 15 PRs merged.

What made the year notable wasn't just the output but the arc. By November, Abhijit had flipped from contributor to mentor, guiding high school students through their first open source contributions on the same codebase — designing tasks, reviewing code, and carrying pre-university students through the full PR lifecycle for Google Code-in. Meanwhile, he was running media and live event coverage for InCTF Junior, the partner university's national cybersecurity CTF for school students, operating as the real-time content arm of a fast-moving live event.

The year closed with selection into the Bertelsmann Technology Scholarship — a competitive global program that funded a Udacity Deep Learning Nanodegree. It was a first, deliberate step toward ML, taken independently and outside of coursework, and a signal of where his interests were beginning to point.

2018

2018

2018 was Abhijit's first year of intentional, outward-facing engineering. The through-line was Android development — learning it, applying it, and sharing it.

The year's most formative thread was open source. Motivated by a long-term goal of participating in Google Summer of Code, Abhijit began browsing the GSoC project archive and landed on Apache Fineract CN — an Android field-agent app for microfinance built under the Apache Software Foundation. Two pull requests followed: a RecyclerView adapter bug fix and a README cleanup, both merged. Small contributions technically, but a meaningful first step into professional open-source norms — commit conventions, PR reviews, Apache project culture.

Alongside that, Abhijit competed in his first hackathon: the satellite-navigation hackathon. Working as part of a team, he built an Android app that used GPS/GNSS data to profile driver behavior — flagging frequent traversal of dangerous terrain as an insurance risk signal. No prize, but a first taste of building under time pressure for a real problem domain.

The year closed with an unexpected teaching moment: in collaboration with Kortline, Abhijit led an Android/Kotlin workshop at a nearby community college. Over 200 students attended — a strong signal that the skills he was building had real demand around him.

Taken together, 2018 was the year Abhijit moved from learning to doing: first OSS commit, first hackathon, first time in front of a room.
